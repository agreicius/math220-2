We will not prove (1), which is the more difficult part of this theorem. Assuming \((1)\) is true, we prove (2) using the chain rule as follows. By (2) of TheoremÂ 1.10.7, we have \(f^{-1}(f(x))=x\) for all \(x\) in the domain of \(f\text{.}\) Taking derivatives of both sides of this equation, we see that
\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx} (f^{-1}(f(x)))=\frac{d}{dx} x\amp \implies (f^{-1})'(f(x))f'(x)=1 \amp \text{(chain rule)}\\ \amp \implies f^{-1}(f(x))=\frac{1}{f'(x)} \amp (f'(x)\ne 0)\text{.} \end{align*}
Equivalently, since for any \(b\in J\) we have \(f^{-1}(b)=a\) for some unique \(a\in I\) satisfying \(f(a)=b\text{,}\) we see that
\begin{align*} (f^{-1})'(b) \amp = (f^{-1})'(f(a)) \\ \amp = \frac{1}{f'(a)} \amp \text{(by above)}\\ \amp = \frac{1}{f'(f^{-1}(b))} \amp (a=f^{-1}(b))\text{.} \end{align*}
