  1. We have \(-\sin t\, \sqrt{\cos t}=f(g(t))g'(t)\) where \(f(t)=\sqrt{t}\) and \(g(t)=\cos t\text{.}\) Since \(F(t)=\frac{2}{3}\sqrt{x}\) is an antiderivative of \(f\text{,}\) Theorem 1.7.1 tells us that \(F(g(t))=\frac{2}{3}(\cos t)^{3/2}\) is an antiderivative of \(f(g(x))g'(x)= -\sin t\, \sqrt{\cos t}\text{.}\) We conclude
    \begin{equation*} \int -\sin t\, \sqrt{\cos t}\, dt =\frac{2}{3}(\cos t)^{3/2}+C\text{.} \end{equation*}
  2. We have \(\frac{\sin(\sqrt{s})}{2\sqrt{s}}=f(g(s))g'(s)\) where \(f(s)=\sqrt{s}\) and \(g(s)=\sqrt{s}\text{.}\) Since \(F(s)=-\cos t\) is an antiderivative of \(f\text{,}\) Theorem 1.7.1 tells us that \(F(g(s))=-\cos(\sqrt{s})\) is an antiderivative of \(f(g(x))g'(x)= \frac{\sin(\sqrt{s})}{2\sqrt{s}}\text{.}\) We conclude
    \begin{equation*} \int \frac{\sin(\sqrt{s})}{2\sqrt{s}}\, ds =-\cos(\sqrt{s})+C\text{.} \end{equation*}
  3. A similar approach as above can be applied, though here we have \(x^2\sqrt{x^3+1}=\frac{1}{3}f(g(x))g'(x)\text{,}\) where \(f(x)=\sqrt{x}\) and \(g(x)=x^3+1\text{.}\) It follows that
    \begin{equation*} \int x^2\sqrt{x^3+1}\, dx=\frac{1}{3}\int f(g(x))g'(x)\, dx=\frac{2}{9}(x^3+1)^{3/2}+C\text{,} \end{equation*}
    since \(F(x)=\frac{2}{3}x^{3/2}\) is an antiderivative of \(f(x)=\sqrt{x}\text{.}\)
