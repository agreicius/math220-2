Example 1.25.8. Limit comparison test.

Decide whether \(\displaystyle \int_0^{1/2}\frac{1}{\sqrt{x}-x}\, dx\text{.}\)
Solution.
This example is designed to force us to use the limit comparison test: because of the minus sign in the denominator, we cannot make use of any useful inequality that would allow us to use the direct comparison test. Note that for \(x\in (0,1/2]\) we have \(\sqrt{x}> x\text{,}\) and thus the integrand is continuous on \((0,1/2]\text{.}\) Our intuition is that the \(\sqrt{x}\) term dominates as \(x\to 0^+\text{,}\) and thus that \(f(x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{x}-x}\) is limit-comparable to \(g(x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{x}}\) as \(x\to 0^+\text{.}\) Letâ€™s confirm this:
\begin{align*} \lim_{x\to 0^+}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \amp = \lim_{x\to 0^+} \frac{\sqrt{x}}{\sqrt{x}-x} \\ \amp = \lim_{x\to 0^+}\frac{1}{1-\sqrt{x}}\\ \amp =\frac{1}{1-0}\\ \amp = 1\text{.} \end{align*}
This proves that \(f\) and \(g\) are limit-comparable as \(x\to 0^+\text{.}\) Since \(\int_0^{1/2} \frac{1}{\sqrt{x}}\, dx\) converges by the type-II \(p\)-test, we conclude that \(\int_0^{1/2}f(x)\, dx\) converges.
