Let temperature \(T\) be given by the function \(T=f(t)\text{.}\) The rate of change of \(T\) with respect to \(t\) is then \(f'(t)\text{.}\) Newtonâ€™s law says this rate of change is proportional to the difference \(f(t)-T_0\) of the current temperature of the object and the room temperature. Mathematically, we write this proportionality relation as
\begin{equation} f'(t)=k(f(t)-T_0)\text{,}\tag{1.13.7} \end{equation}
where \(k\) is some fixed constant, called the constant of proportionality.
